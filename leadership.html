Leadership RTFM
These are free-form personal journals and reviews of leadership material. No book must be primarily about leadership to hold merit. The best often advertise nothing of the sort, just like people ;).

Axioms

If you don't read, you can't lead.

Courage isn't having the strength to go on - it is going on when you don't have strength.

Value initiative and aggressiveness above all. It is easier to pull the reins back than to push a timid soul forward ... you don't control your subordinate commanders' every move; you clearly state your intent and unleash their initiative.

Fortitudine Vincimus

To obstruct eachother is against Nature's law -- and what is irritation or aversion but a form of obstruction?

Lit

To quote General Mattis, "If you don't read, you can't lead."

  • Call Sign Chaos, General Jim Mattis

    I admire Mattis' calling for leaders to 'protect their mavericks and risk takers, and reward initiative and aggressiveness'. For Marines, aggressiveness clearly refers to those exercising the strongest initiative, such as youthful itinerants like young Mattis himself. Progress is impossible when organizations are too timid to break s***, including organizational lassitude. Innovation requires aggressiveness. But leaders must temper zeal with self-discipline and reading. To not read is to forego the acquired knowledge for which previous leaders believed it was worthwhile to record their experiences. Thus if I could summarize Mattis' leadership principles, albeit inaccurately:

    1. Vertical alignment by intended outcomes, rather than detailed directives: "in order to take the bridge" "in order to deny egress" "in order to _________"
    2. Lateral aggressiveness and competency
    3. Learn from your predecessors, read voraciously

    Mattis spoke to a packed auditorium at my old university and his appeal is easy to understand. The Marines' leadership mindset is well-compartmentalized: you have a role, you understand your role, you fulfill your role. Combined with the values of fraternity, self-discipline, and learning/adapting/exercising, it is easy to understand why that mindset is infectious, and likewise dovetails with Aurelius' stoicism. Solely for emphasizing reading and listening, Mattis distinguishes himself as a leader capable of much greater public significance. I wish to hear more of him in the future. When speaking on leadership and democracy, he does so without engaging in the commonplace self-dealing prevalent within universities, media, and corporations, a core reason for their decreasing national relevance.

  • Endurance, Alfred Lansing

    The greatest account of determination against oarlocks, the Shackleton expedition's survival is arguably misattributed to Shackleton himself rather than his team. The sequence of hardships they experienced exemplify Mattis' 'direct leadership' as a chain of short-term contexts and decisions made under environmental uncertainty to achieve a single long-term objective: saving as many crew as possible. Shackleton did so by keeping problematic crew members nearer himself, but otherwise unleashing the capabilities of a skilled team to operate independently and effectively. This is a credit to the team's hand-picked composition, experts in their trades except for one stowaway. In their moment, such men could not know what outcome might follow, only that positive outcomes would proceed solely from their own actions. There is no luck but hard luck, but that is the best we have.

    Survival narratives struggle against readers' natural psychological resistance to fully contemplate a context in which absolute mortal consequences and uncertainty are pitted against the personal commitment (and a difficult one) to act toward survival: the acceptance of one's situation and the conscious choice to behave rationally and agreeably toward difficult goals. The fascinating part about the team was their cheery acceptance of circumstance, and the collective understanding of each man's value and responsibilities, including the responsibility not to share spoiling attitudes in the face of uncertainty. No bad situation is chosen, but good outcomes can only extend from choice.

  • The CIA's Greatest Covert Operation: Inside the Daring Mission to Recover a Nuclear-Armed Soviet Sub , David H. Sharp

    Once noticed, it can never be unnoticed that SERIOUS CAPS titles are required for admission to the military history section at your local book store. Because believe it or not, it is a lesser known fact of history (by 2022 standards anyhow) that T.E. Lawrence would today be a highly self-serious Tom Clancy ghostwriter, and Wilfred Owen an operator of several 'osint' twitter accounts (otherwise known as 'googling') for re-posting slick drone warfare videos from a tony hot cocoa nook in DC. 'Dulce et decorum' that, Ghost of Kiev... Though slobbering second-hand military history narratives make good-faith attempts to memorialize major national events, they often disappoint because of the loss of gritty firsthand knowledge, very much including the vulgarity of real events and their participants. Murphy's law is a democracy of demons, in combat as much as engineering, and doesn't reduce to solo-hero narratives. Perhaps autocratic societies still have bars because ultimately people can still distinguish hard truth from Falstaffian puffery.

    So nevermind that Sharp's book hails from the CAPITAL LETTERS section. Nor that much of the story may be deliberate fabrication. It provided something I'd never encountered: a professional engineering memoir. Except that this memoir is about—well—stealing a sunken Russian submarine: engineering multiple massive and unproven custom apparatuses, coordinating front companies and stories, Howard Hughes Corp, ops-sec lapses, Soviet surveillance, and a large-scale project with mixed outcomes (familiar to any large software contract). Most of us should stick to mastering the 25¢ claw game at Safeway, but I hope Sharp kept the 240z and the K-129 fialka in the glovebox.

    Given my own fascination with Project Azorian, the interesting part of Sharp's account is that the spy intrigue subsided and yielded to a professional project memoir that is relatable to anyone involved in a large-scale engineering project. All such endeavors yield mixed outcomes and survivor-guilt levels of personal second-guessing. Every engineer should cringe when reading about the moment the heave-compensator slammed to its stops. The discipline required to carry through such an extensive multi-year project is admirable in itself, and one envies its field work. The scale of Azorian was an amazing undertaking, unique for its managerial and engineering requirements. It ought to be more well-known to the public as a major Cold War military-engineering accomplishment. The beauty of Sharp's memoir is that in an era when professional responsibility and leadership are openly diminished, Azorian exemplified the absolute professional obligations faced by engineers, which is perhaps what makes the work so interesting and healthy. I look forward to seeing it on the big screen, because even a mediocre production would be entertaining given the merits of Azorian itself.

  • Turn the Ship Around!: A True Story of Turning Followers into Leaders, L. David Marquet

    'Turning followers into leaders' oozes TED Talk cliches. But on reflection, followership forms the basis of every social media platform, most professional credentials, and 99% of LinkedIn (which I ditched for exactly this reason). There is even a book of the same title promoting 'followership' with all the false optimism of early-oughts marketing 3.0 optimism. Marquet instead instructs us to use language to substitute subordination ("Sir, may I ____") with intent: "Sir, I intend to ____". Conscientiously structuring language is the low-hanging fruit of leadership praxis, a subtle team contract that a leader is someone who creates other leaders. Marquet is a perfect complement to Mattis. Whereas Mattis' vertical leadership principle emphasizes communicating intent downward, Marquet's principle emphasizes pushing subordinates upward by harnessing their 'intent' and building up their (autonomous) initiative. This appropriately decouples leaders and subordinates, notably without a bunch of obtuse language about 'decentralization'. Marquet does not seek to replace leadership hierarchy but to ensure that it exists to maximize initiative and the creation of new leaders.

    Perhaps the main theme of Marquet's book is to encompass an entire organization in leadership, and to not yield to reductive authority roles. A somewhat common example, I worked at an organization where favoritism and dubious dev point metrics resulted in some workmates disproportionately receiving greenfield tasks, thus building their skillsets while starving others equally qualified. Like all such non-linear point schemes, the scoring system merely encouraged developers to assign themselves easy tasks and discouraged more difficult ones, which by definition can be nearly impossible to estimate (i.e. bug hunts). The result was that many colleagues adopted an attitude of subordination, precisely because, being downstream of the objectives, they were essentially being required to ask permission to do their work, as well as being discouraged from doing the real work of quality and security (essentially, the core problem that dev-sec-ops intends to address). Projects became progressively more buggy because the point system disincentivized addressing quality issues, while favoring the proliferation of new and undocumented code. And without greenfield tasks, downstream workmates did not adequately develop their own creative intuition about how things could be done, but were pinioned by what already had been done, and was very often done poorly. Many developers seemingly did not advance their skillsets or experience, despite doing lots of apparent work. Several moved on to better companies. Likewise, providing insightful feedback regarding machine learning applications (a core product domain), security, or open source dev productivity tooling was regarded with suspicion by leads for being 'out of one's lane' or a threat to their kingdom. Virtually the entire company's software operations were structured in this manner, as silo'ed, feuding kingdoms, a matrix of rivalries that produced little for its partipants and turned the software into internally-focused and awkwardly overlapping mishmash (Conway's law).

    This team organization formed a self-fulfilling hierarchy of role-acquiescence. Engineers' capabilities exceeded the constraints created by upstream bottleneckers, but the company culture presented toxic subservience as 'being a team player' and other cringe cliches. Nor would I hesitate to work with any of those team members again, whom I know to be bright and sensitive to criticism; I moved on for unrelated reasons. But without encouraging subordinates to develop their own leadership skills and technical autonomy, and without cultivating an attitude that leadership may arise from any team member, capabilities are supplanted by subordination and a culture of asking permission. I think of that experience solely in terms of lost capabilities and advancement, and the weeks spent fixing others' bugs instead of developing relevant skills and valuable products. My final year consisted almost entirely of re-work of others mistakes, rather than actual work. The experience is commonplace in an industry ruled by technical debt, but provides a stark contrast to the Shackleton approach of assembling team members for autonomy, not obedience to self-preserving hierarchy. It's always strange that highly intelligent people can be so adept at role-acceptance as such (I am as guilty as anyone else). Marquet's book is a refreshing antidote, especially in the tech industry. A welcome read, and a book that should probably be re-read periodically.

    Damn fine book. Zamperini's persistence is a terrific source of motivation and a reminder of the opportunity we enjoy because of his generation. It is also a contrast to how much the culture of Japan has transformed since the 19th and early-20th centuries.

  • Servant Leadership, Robert K. Greenleaf

    Servant Leadership reads more like Kant than leadership pulp. Whereas the latter is flatters its readership to pump up book sales, Servant Leadership is valuable precisely because it requires effort to grasp. Rather than tossing a cape on every middle-manager, bored administrator, or appealing to readers' narcissisism, Servant Leadership represents an omega-male's earnest attempt to understand authority-based leadership. What makes Servant Leadership so current is that its message is retrograde to the withered, self-serving realizations of leadership in 2020's America. However if you look for the servant-leaders within any organization, you discover their actual structure and capabilities. I once implemented a network-security project analyzing organizational hierarchy in the Enron dataset and several employees within the company were easily identified as critical servant-leaders, without whom the company could not have operated (otherwise ignoring Enron's fate). They did not bear titles or rank within the company that matched the importance of their roles. Servant-leaders exist within every organization and provide key operational insights. A quote from Greenleaf's site summarizes the core qualities of servant-leadership:

    The difference manifests itself in the care taken by the servant-first to make sure that other people's highest priority needs are being served. The best test, and difficult to administer, is: Do those served grow as persons? Do they, while being served, become healthier, wiser, freer, more autonomous, more likely themselves to become servants? And, what is the effect on the least privileged in society? Will they benefit or at least not be further deprived?

    Servant-leadership is an explication of Marquet's belief that a leader is one who creates other leaders:

    • it is evaluated based on the production of new leaders and autonomous action
    • it emphasizes horizontal definitions of service
    • it refutes the utility of vertical hierarchies which diminish autonomy

    Greenleaf often strays into the arcadian weeds when praising a kind of horizontal servant-leadership organization akin to every "empowerment" fad crossing one's career feeds. The arc of institutions pursuing these agenda have historically concluded with the strongest examples of staunch, vertical authoritarianism (see Orwell's Animal Farm or any 2020's university administration). Though I subscribe to servant-leadership in principle, I reject Greenleaf's aesthetic. Or can it even have one? There was this one guy 2000 years ago who mentioned something about the corrosive societal consequences of doing good deeds for recognition, versus doing so without reciprocation and without a witness. Although I love the concept of servant-leadership (as someone who has occupied the role in many companies I've worked for) I can leave the 1970's boomer-identity gospel and keep the practical bits. Vertical hierarchy is not an intrinsic evil; it can easily be argued that horizontal servant-leadership and vertical authority should simply be balanced.

    Peer within any company, no matter its health (hence the Enron example), and you will find core staff who sincerely believe in its survival, mission, and purpose. Their titles are often a mismatch with respect to their (usually unobserved) operational importance. But were they taught to do so? Can servant leadership be taught, as every quasi-leadership "empowerment" project suggests? I would be as radical as to claim that, for several generations now, America has lacked any prior reference that would make servant-leadership intelligible to its intended audience. It will be intelligible to those who already inately possess the value, for whom no training is needed, and otherwise just another lever for covert self-service.

    So can it be realized, or it just the latest organizational fetish on LinkedIn? I once had a friend who became enamoured with outdoor magazines and fancied himself a mountaineer adventurer. When we climbed together, he was completely incompetent and would easily become lost, mislead others, and fall into streams, and was also unwilling to learn basic skills. Evidently to be enamoured with skills does not impart them. In mountaineering it became routine to encounter such dangerous dilletantes, as a few of them put themselves or others in critical danger and in need of rescue. Woke'ish leadership crusades represent a similar confusion between skill and the work that begets it, diminishing the work out of envy for skill: genuine leadership skills are not a thing in-themselves to be bought like some transferrable currency or personal status product (Teslas, iphones, etc).

    Skill is an expression of work, and of an individual's commitment to surmount the burdens of a task, subject, etc. The assumption that it might be easily transferrable reflects a fundamental misunderstanding of the long-term requirements of leadership, and a seeming violation of servant-leadership's core ethos itself, which is that leadership entails doing work. In that regard, and not to be too stereotypical, who are servant leaders but plumbers and janitors? By contrast, what then of the credential spigot of a higher education system awash in elitism, for which every accomodation and recognition must be made, no matter how frivolous or unyielding of company value? The entire premise of paying for a college degree (particularly advanced degrees in law, management, and STEM) is sold in overtly classist terms: 'a degree gains you entry to the over-class'. Thereby, the wokies decrying matters of class are the ones affirming it. The very concept of this saintly insider class--programmers, lawyers, directors, etc.--contradicts the notion of work: how does a diploma give one permission to work? Or, let's be honest here, is it really an implied exemption from work? What is the survival timeline of servant leadership in this era of near programmatic mimetic-envy? Of eased character, and diminished leadership? These are controversial but necessary questions for Greenleaf.

  • Gates of Fire, Steven Pressfield

    Gates of Fire can be a bit of an over-the-top account of Spartan soldiers written from the perspective of a tightjawed New England spectator of 'hardship'. But for anyone of a harder upbringing, those whose teens and twenties were a long train of hardship jobs and thankless trades skills, or those who privately cringe whenever the LinkedIn crowd slings terms like "grit", this book is an enjoyable foray into the past hardship that secretly motivate oneself.
