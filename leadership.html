Leadership RTFM

Fortitudine Vincimus

These are free-form personal meditations and reviews of leadership material. No book must be primarily about leadership to hold value. The best often advertise nothing of the sort.

Axioms

If you don't read, you can't lead.

Courage isn't having the strength to go on - it is going on when you don't have strength.

Value initiative and aggressiveness above all. It is easier to pull the reins back than to push a timid soul forward ... you don't control your subordinate commanders' every move; you clearly state your intent and unleash their initiative.

Lit

To quote General Mattis, "If you don't read, you can't lead."

  • Call Sign Chaos, General Jim Mattis

    I admire Mattis' calling for leaders to 'protect their mavericks and risk takers, and reward initiative and aggressiveness'. For Marines, aggressiveness clearly refers to those exercising the strongest initiative, such as youthful itinerants like young Mattis himself. Progress is impossible when organizations are too timid to break s***, including organizational lassitude. Innovation requires aggressiveness. But leaders must temper zeal with self-discipline and reading. To not read is to forego the acquired knowledge for which previous leaders believed it was worthwhile to record their experiences. Thus if I could summarize Mattis' leadership principles, albeit inaccurately:

    1. Vertical alignment by of intended outcomes, rather than detailed directives: "in order to take the bridge" "in order to deny egress" "in order to _________"
    2. Lateral aggressiveness and competency
    3. Learn from your predecessors, read voraciously

    Mattis spoke to a packed auditorium at my old university and his appeal is easy to understand. The Marines' leadership mindset is well-compartmentalized: you have a role, you understand your role, you fulfill your role. Combined with the values of fraternity, self-discipline, and learning/adapting/exercising, it is easy to understand why that mindset is infectious, and likewise dovetails with Aurelius' stoicism. Solely for emphasizing reading and listening, Mattis distinguishes himself as a leader capable of much greater public significance. I wish to hear more of him in the future. When speaking on leadership and democracy, he does so without engaging in the commonplace self-dealing prevalent within universities, media, and corporations, a core reason for their decreasing national relevance.

  • Endurance, Alfred Lansing

    The greatest account of determination against the oarlocks, the Shackleton expedition's survival is arguably misattributed to Shackleton himself rather than his team. The sequence of hardships they experienced exemplify Mattis' 'direct leadership' as a chain of short-term contexts and decisions made under environmental uncertainty to achieve a single long-term objective: saving as many crew as possible. Shackleton did so by keeping problematic crew members nearer himself, but otherwise unleashing the capabilities of a skilled team to operate independently and effectively. This is a credit to the team's hand-picked composition, experts in their trades except for one stowaway. In their moment, such men could not know what outcome might follow, but that positive outcomes might solely follow from their own actions. There is no luck but hard luck, but that is the best we have.

    Survival narratives struggle against readers' natural psychological resistance to fully comprehend a context in which absolute mortal consequences and uncertainty are pitted against the personal choice (and it is a difficult choice) to act toward survival: the acceptance of one's situation and the conscious choice to act rationally and agreeably toward difficult goals. The fascinating part about the team was their apparent cheery acceptance of circumstance, and the collective understanding of each man's responsibilities and value, including the responsibility not to share spoiling attitudes in the face of uncertainty. No bad situation is ever chosen, but good outcomes extend purely from choice.

  • Meditations, Marcus Aurelius

    You can't control the world, but you can exercise absolute control over how you respond to it. Meditations is a masterpiece that speaks for itself.

  • The CIA's Greatest Covert Operation: Inside the Daring Mission to Recover a Nuclear-Armed Soviet Sub , David H. Sharp

    Once noticed, it can never be unnoticed that SERIOUS CAPS titles are required for admission to the military history section at your local book store. Because believe it or not, it is a lesser known fact of history (by 2022 standards anyhow) that T.E. Lawrence would today be a highly self-serious Tom Clancy ghostwriter, and Wilfred Owen an operator of several 'osint' twitter accounts (otherwise known as googling stuff) for re-posting slick drone warfare videos from a cozy hot cocoa nook in DC. Dulce et Decorum est, Ghost of Kiev. Although slobbering second-hand narratives put forth good-faith attempts to memorialize major national events, they soon disappoint because of the loss of gritty firsthand knowledge, very much including the vulgarity of real events and their participants. Maybe the reason untruthful societies have bars is because ultimately the people still recognize truth from Falstaffian mythos.

    So nevermind that Sharp's book hails from the CAPITAL LETTERS section. It provided something I had never encountered, an engineering memoir. Except that this memoir is about—well—stealing a sunken Russian submarine: engineering multiple massive and unproven custom apparatuses, coordinating front companies and stories, Howard Hughes Corp, ops-sec lapses, Soviet surveillance, and a large-scale project with mixed outcomes (familiar to any large software contract). Most of us should stick to mastering the 25¢ claw game at Safeway, but I hope Sharp kept the 240z and the K-129 fialka in the glovebox.

    Given my initial fascination with Project Azorian, the most interesting part of Sharp's account is that the spy intrigue subsided, yielding to a professional project memoir that is highly relatable to anyone involved in a large-scale project. All such endeavors yield mixed outcomes and survivor-guilt levels of long-term personal second-guessing. The discipline required to carry through such an extensive multi-year project is admirable in itself, and I envy its field work. The scale of Azorian was an amazing undertaking, unique for its managerial and engineering requirements. Azorian ought to be more well-known to the public as a major military-engineering accomplishment of the Cold War.

  • Turn the Ship Around!: A True Story of Turning Followers into Leaders, L. David Marquet

    'Turning followers into leaders' oozes with TED Talk cliches. But on reflection, followership forms the basis of every social media platform, most professional credentials, and 99% of LinkedIn (which I ditched for exactly this reason). There is even a book promoting it. Marquet instructs us to instead use language to substitute subordination ("Sir, may I ____") with intent: "Sir, I intend to ____". Conscientiously structuring language is the low-hanging fruit of leadership praxis, forming a subtle team contract that a leader is someone who creates other leaders and respects subordinates' initiative. Marquet is a perfect complement to Mattis. Whereas Mattis' vertical leadership principle emphasizes communicating intent downward, Marquet's principle emphasizes pushing subordinates upward by harnessing their 'intent' and building up their (autonomous) initiative. This appropriately decouples leaders and subordinates, notably without a bunch of self-contradictory language about 'decentralization'. Marquet does not seek to replace leadership hierarchy but to ensure that it exists to maximize initiative and the creation of new leaders.

    Perhaps the main themes of Marquet's book are to encompass an entire organization in leadership, and to not yield to reductive authority roles. A somewhat common example, I worked at an organization where favoritism and dubious dev point metrics resulted in some workmates receiving more (if not all) greenfield tasks, thus building their skillsets, while starving others equally qualified. Like most 'point' schemes, the scoring system merely encouraged developers to assign themselves easy tasks and discouraged more difficult ones, which by definition can be nearly impossible to estimate (i.e. bug hunts). The result was that many colleagues adopted an attitude of subordination, precisely because they were being required to ask permission to do their work. Code projects became progressively more buggy because the point system disincentivized addressing quality issues, but favored lots of new and undocumented code. Without greenfield tasks, downstream workmates did not adequately develop their own creative intuition about how things could be done, but were pinioned by what already had been done, and was very often done poorly. Likewise, providing insightful feedback regarding machine learning applications (a core product domain), security, or open source dev productivity tooling was regarded with suspicion by leads for being 'out of one's lane' or a threat to their kingdom. Virtually the entire company's software operations were structured as silo'ed, feuding kingdoms.

    This team organization formed a self-fulfilling hierarchy of role acceptance, whereby engineers' capabilities exceeded the constraints created by bottleneckers, but the company culture portrayed toxic subservience as 'being a team player', etc. Nor would I hesitate to work with any of those team members again, whom I know to be bright and sensitive to criticism; I moved on for unrelated reasons. But without encouraging subordinates to develop their own leadership skills and technical autonomy, and without cultivating an attitude that leadership should arise from any team member, capabilities are supplanted by subordination and a culture of asking permission. I often think of that experience in terms of the lost capabilities and advancement, and the weeks spent fixing others' bugs instead of developing relevant skills and valuable products. The experience is commonplace in an industry ruled by technical debt, but provides a stark contrast to the Shackleton approach of assembling team members for autonomy, not obedience to self-preserving hierarchy. I always thought it strange that highly intelligent people can be so adept at role-acceptance as such (I am as guilty as anyone, if not the intelligent part :)). Marquet's book is a refreshing antidote, especially in the tech industry. A welcome read, and a book that should probably be re-read periodically.

  • Unbroken, Laura Hillenbrand

    Damn fine book. Zamperini's persistence is a terrific source of motivation and a reminder of the opportunity we enjoy because of his generation. It is also a contrast to how much the culture of Japan has transformed since the 19th and early-20th centuries.

  • Servant Leadership, Robert K. Greenleaf

    Servant Leadership reads more like Kant than leadership pulp, the latter commonly intended to flatter rather than challenge the reader. But it is a valuable book precisely because it requires labor to grasp. Rather than tossing a cape on every middle-manager, bored administrator, or appealing to readers' narcissistic traits, Servant Leadership represents an earnest attempt to understand authority-based leadership. What makes Servant Leadership so current is that its message is retrograde to the withered, self-serving definitions of leadership in 2020's America. But if you look for the servant-leaders within any organization, you tend to discover its actual structure and capabilities. Oddly I once implemented a a network-security project analyzing organizational hierarchy in the Enron dataset and several employees within that company were easily identified as critical servant-leaders, without whom the company couldn't have operated (otherwise ignoring Enron's fate). They did not bear titles or rank within the company that matched the importance of their roles.

  • Gates of Fire, Steven Pressfield

    Gates of Fire can be a bit of an over-the-top account of Spartan soldiers written from the perspective of a tightjawed spectator of 'hardship'. But for anyone of a harder upbringing, those whose teens and twenties were a long train of hardship jobs and thankless trades skills, or those who privately cringe whenever the LinkedIn crowd slings terms like "grit", this book is an enjoyable foray into the past hardship that can secretly motivate one.
