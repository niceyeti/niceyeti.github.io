Leadership RTFM

Fortitudine vincimus

These are merely personal notes on my leadership reading list. No book must be primarily about leadership, and in fact, the best of the best often advertise nothing of the sort. These are merely free form personal meditations/reviews.

Quotes

If you don't read, you can't lead.
Courage isn't having the strength to go on - it is going on when you don't have strength.
Value initiative and aggressiveness above all.

It is easier to pull the reins back than to push a timid soul forward...

You don't control your subordinate commanders' every move; you clearly state your intent and unleash their initiative.

Lit

To quote General Mattis, "If you don't read, you can't lead." Just be grateful this list is short.

  • Call Sign Chaos, General Jim Mattis

    I admire Mattis' suggestion that leaders should 'protect their mavericks and risk takers, and reward initiative and aggressiveness'. For Marines, 'aggressiveness' clearly refers to those expressing the strongest initiative, likely young itinerants like Mattis himself. Progress is impossible when you're too timid to break s***, including organizational lassitude. Innovation requires aggressiveness. But leaders must also temper zeal with self-discipline and reading: to not read is to forego the reasons previous leaders believed it was worthwhile to record their experiences. If I could summarize Mattis' leadership principles, albeit perhaps incorrectly:

    1. Vertical understanding of objectives ("in order to take _____")
    2. Lateral aggressiveness and competence-coaching

  • Endurance, Alfred Lansing

    The ultimate account of iron determination against the oarlocks, the Shackleton expedition's survival is arguably misattributed to Shackleton himself and not his team. Endurance exemplifies Mattis' 'direct leadership' as a chain of short-term contexts and decisions made under inscrutable environmental uncertainty, to achieve the single long-term objective of saving as many of his crew as possible. Shackleton did so by keeping problematic crew members near himself, but otherwise unleashing the capabilities of a skilled team to operate independently and effectively.

  • Meditations, Marcus Aurelius

    You can't control the world, but you can exercise absolute control over how you respond to it. Meditations speaks for itself.

  • The Art of War, Sun Tzu

    Lol, just joking. I have no idea why this book is on so many reading lists, especially men's oriented reading lists except to communicate some willingness to lie, exploit, and deceive others. Like what? How is that leadership? The Art of War is garbage, or some batman comicbook version of leadership, and is most often meant to communicate willingness to mislead. Yeah... no.

  • The CIA's Greatest Covert Operation: Inside the Daring Mission to Recover a Nuclear-Armed Soviet Sub , David H. Sharp

    Nevermind that this book hails from the CAPITAL LETTERS! military-history section, it provides something unheard of: an engineering memoir. Except that it is a memoir about--well--stealing a Russian submarine, engineering gargantuan pieces of equipment to do so, coordinating front companies and cover stories, Howard Hughes Corp, ops-sec lapses, Soviet surveillance, and a large-scale project with mixed outcomes (in other words, every large software contract). Most of us will stick to mastering the 25 cent claw game at Walmart, but I hope he kept the 240z and the K-129 fialka in the glovebox. Sharp's account is relatable to anyone involved in a large-scale project, which inevitably results in mixed outcomes.

  • Turn the Ship Around!: A True Story of Turning Followers into Leaders, L. David Marquet

    'Turning followers into leaders' drips TED-Talk cliches, but on reflection, followership forms the basis of every social media platform, most professional credentials, and 99% of LinkedIn (which I ditched long ago, for exactly this reason). Marquet's contribution is using language to substitute subordination ("Sir, may I ____") with intent: "Sir, I intend to ____". Structuring language is the low-hanging fruit of leadership praxis, a subtle team contract that a leader is someone who creates other leaders.

  • Unbroken, Laura Hillenbrand

    Damn fine book. Zamperini's persistence is a terrific source of motivation and a reminder of how much opportunity we enjoy because of the WWII generation.

  • Servant Leadership, Robert K. Greenleaf

    Servant Leadership reads more like Kant than leadership pulp, the latter commonly intended to flatter rather than challenge the reader. It is a valuable book precisely because it requires labor to grasp its message. Rather than tossing a cape on every awful middle-manager, bored administrator, or toxic narcissist reader, Servant Leadership represents an earnest attempt to understand authority-based leadership. What makes Servant Leadership so current is that its message is retrograde to the withered, self-serving definitions of leadership in 2020's America. If you look for the servant-leaders within any organization, you will find its actual structure and capabilities.

  • Gates of Fire, Steven Pressfield

    Gates of Fire can be a bit of an over-the-top account of Spartan soldiers written from the perspective of a tightjawed perspectator of 'hardship'. But for anyone of a harder upbringing, those whose teens and twenties were a long train of hardship jobs and thankless trades skills, or those who privately cringe whenever their superiors sling terms like "synergy", this book is an enjoyable foray into the past hardship that can secretly motivate one.
