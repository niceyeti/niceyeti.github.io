Leadership RTFM

Fortitudine vincimus

These are jottings on personal leadership readings. No book must be primarily about leadership to hold value, and the best often advertise nothing of the sort. These are merely free form personal meditations/reviews.

Axioms

If you don't read, you can't lead.

Courage isn't having the strength to go on - it is going on when you don't have strength.

Value initiative and aggressiveness above all. It is easier to pull the reins back than to push a timid soul forward ... you don't control your subordinate commanders' every move; you clearly state your intent and unleash their initiative.

Lit

To quote General Mattis, "If you don't read, you can't lead." Pity this list is not longer!

  • Call Sign Chaos, General Jim Mattis

    I admire Mattis' calling for leaders to 'protect their mavericks and risk takers, and reward initiative and aggressiveness'. For the Marines, 'aggressiveness' clearly refers to those exercising the strongest initiative, such as youthful itinerants like young Mattis himself. Progress is impossible when organizations are too timid to break s***, including organizational lassitude. Innovation requires aggressiveness. But leaders must also temper zeal with self-discipline and reading: to not read is to forego the reasons previous leaders believed it worthwhile to record their experience. If I could summarize Mattis' leadership principles, albeit inaccurately:

    1. Vertical understanding of objectives' intent, not details and directives: "in order to take the bridge" "in order to deny egress" "in order to _________"
    2. Lateral aggressiveness and competency
    3. Learn from your predecessors, read voraciously

    Mattis spoke to a packed auditorium at my old university and his appeal is easy to understand. The Marines' leadership mindset is well-compartmentalized: you have a role, you understand your role, you fulfill your role. Combined with the values of fraternity, self-discipline, and learning/exercising, it is easy to understand why that mindset is infectious, and likewise dovetails with Aurelius' stoicism. By emphasizing reading and listening, Mattis distinguishes him as a leader capable of greater public significance than retirement to the arcadia of beltway thinktanks and universities.

  • Endurance, Alfred Lansing

    The best account of determination against the oarlocks, the Shackleton expedition's survival is arguably misattributed to Shackleton himself rather than his team. Their hardships exemplify Mattis' 'direct leadership' as a chain of short-term contexts and decisions made under environmental uncertainty to achieve a single long-term objective, saving his crew. Shackleton did so by keeping problematic crew members nearer himself, but otherwise unleashing the capabilities of a skilled team to operate independently and effectively. This is a credit to the composition of his team (but for one stowaway), all experts in their trades. The fascinating part about the Shackleton team was their apparent cheery acceptance of circumstance, and the collective understanding of each man's responsibilities and value, including their responsibility not to share spoiling attitudes.

  • Meditations, Marcus Aurelius

    You can't control the world, but you can exercise absolute control over how you respond to it. Meditations speaks for itself.

  • The CIA's Greatest Covert Operation: Inside the Daring Mission to Recover a Nuclear-Armed Soviet Sub , David H. Sharp

    Once noticed, it can never be unnoticed that ALL CAPS TITLES! are required for admission to the military history section of your local book store. Believe it or not, it is a lesser known fact of history (by 2022 standards anyhow) that T.E. Lawrence himself would be a highly self-serious Tom Clancy ghostwriter, and Wilfred Owen an operator of several 'osint' accounts (otherwise known as 'googling stuff') for posting slick drone warfare videos from a tony D.C. walkup.

    So nevermind that this book hails from the CAPITAL LETTERS section, it provided something I'd never encountered, an engineering memoir. Except, this memoir is about--well--stealing a sunken Russian submarine: engineering multiple massive custom apparatuses to do so, coordinating front companies and stories, Howard Hughes Corp, ops-sec lapses, Soviet surveillance, and a large-scale project with mixed outcomes (in other words, any large software contract). Most of us will stick to mastering the 25 cent claw game at Walmart, but I hope he kept the 240z and the K-129 fialka in the glovebox. Sharp's account is relatable to anyone involved in a large-scale project, all of which inevitably yield mixed outcomes and survivor-guilt levels of long term personal second-guessing. I admire the discipline required to carry through such an extensive multi-year project, and I envy its field work. Azorian was an amazing large-scale undertaking, admirable for its unique management requirements, and ought to be more well-known to the public.

  • Turn the Ship Around!: A True Story of Turning Followers into Leaders, L. David Marquet

    'Turning followers into leaders' drips TED-Talk cliches. But on reflection, followership forms the basis of every social media platform, most professional credentials, and 99% of LinkedIn (which I ditched for exactly this reason). There is even a book promoting it. Marquet's instruction is instead to use language to substitute subordination ("Sir, may I ____") with intent: "Sir, I intend to ____". Structuring language is the low-hanging fruit of leadership praxis, a subtle team contract that a leader is someone who creates other leaders. Besides the point about language, perhaps the main theme of Marquet's book is not to allow reductive authority to diminish leadership. Marquet is likely a complement to Mattis. Whereas Mattis' vertical leadership principle emphasizes communicating intent downward, Marquet's principle emphasizes pushing subordinates upward by harnessing their 'intent' and building up their (autonomous) initiative. This appropriately decouples leaders and subordinates, notably without a bunch of nonsensical language about 'decentralization'.

  • Unbroken, Laura Hillenbrand

    Damn fine book. Zamperini's persistence is a terrific source of motivation and a reminder of how much opportunity we enjoy because of his generation.

  • Servant Leadership, Robert K. Greenleaf

    Servant Leadership reads more like Kant than leadership pulp, the latter commonly intended to flatter rather than challenge the reader. But it is a valuable book precisely because it requires labor to grasp. Rather than tossing a cape on every middle-manager, bored administrator, or narcissistic reader, Servant Leadership represents an earnest attempt to understand authority-based leadership. What makes Servant Leadership so current is that its message is retrograde to the withered, self-serving definitions of leadership in 2020's America. If you look for the servant-leaders within any organization, you tend to discover its actual structure and capabilities. Oddly I once implemented a a network-security project analyzing organizational hierarchy in the Enron dataset and several employees within that company were easily identified as critical servant-leaders, without whom the company couldn't have operated (otherwise ignoring Enron's fate).

  • Gates of Fire, Steven Pressfield

    Gates of Fire can be a bit of an over-the-top account of Spartan soldiers written from the perspective of a tightjawed spectator of 'hardship'. But for anyone of a harder upbringing, those whose teens and twenties were a long train of hardship jobs and thankless trades skills, or those who privately cringe whenever their superiors sling terms like "grit", this book is an enjoyable foray into the past hardship that can secretly motivate one.
